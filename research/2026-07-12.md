## TOPIC 1: China Coffee Market — July 20 Open Window is REAL, But Uganda Moved First

### Key Findings

**The Policy**
- **July 20, 2026:** China opens to coffee beans from all 53 African countries under a unified phytosanitary framework.
- Zero tariffs on African coffee already active (eliminated mid-2025). FOB price is the only barrier.
- Single approval replaces 53 separate country-by-country protocols — administrative friction just collapsed.

**Who's Buying in China**
- **Luckin Coffee** — 20,000+ stores, world's largest coffee chain by store count. Looking for volume, not specialty. Rwanda washed A1 fits their house blend at ~USD 4.80/kg FOB.
- **Tim Hortons China** — 500+ stores, Canadian chain with strong instant/mild roast preference. Larger particle = A2 natural grade viable.
- **JD.com / FreshHema** — E-commerce import channels. These are the most realistic first contacts for a small exporter without a China office.
- **Heytea** — Premium tea chain, not ideal for green coffee. Skip.
- Uganda is already actively courting Chinese buyers — Coffee Bash, Alibaba-matched buyers, MICE platform — Rwanda needs to move fast before China buyers default to Uganda as their known African origin story.

**China Buyer Reality Check**
- Chinese roasters prefer **container-scale orders** (1,000kg+) — 60kg bags are small. Price must compete on cup quality, not unit price.
- Trust is the core barrier. A South African intermediary with PROWTC / Rainforest Alliance certs is a credibility signal. Rwanda origin is well-regarded (single-origin premium).
- CIIE November 2026 (Shanghai) — still the single best opportunity to meet buyers face-to-face. Booth cost ~USD 3,000-8,000. ROI likely high if Luckin or JD.com buyers are met.

**UAE / GCC — Headwinds Right Now**
- Middle East coffee market: $11.5B, growing — but GCC facing disruptions: Red Sea shipping reroutes add 2-3 weeks, freight costs up significantly, tourism demand softer.
- Hotel groups remain reliable buyers — they need continuous supply regardless of market noise. Specialty is secondary to reliability.
- **Freight disruption favors proximity suppliers.** Ethiopian and Kenyan coffee (via Djibouti/Red Sea) face longer routes. South Africa via Durban is a safer route for SA-roasted Rwandan green bean.

## TOPIC 2: SA B2B Coffee — Hotels, Restaurants, Offices: Pain Points Are the Opportunity

### Key Findings

**Market Size**
- SA out-of-home coffee market: ~$1.94B (2026), growing at 3.14% CAGR.
- B2B wholesale = 63% of global coffee market. Hotels, restaurants, offices, healthcare, education — these are the volume buyers.
- SA specialty coffee market is growing fast — more cafés opening in JHB/CPT, events driving interest. But supply chain fragmentation is the persistent complaint.

**Who the Buyers Are + How They Source**
- **Hotels (4-5 star):** Procurement via Group Purchasing Organizations (GPOs) like Avendra, or direct from large distributors (Tribe, Fiusis). Contracts 1-2 years. Price-insensitive on quality, highly sensitive on reliability. Need: 24hr supply backup, consistent grind calibration, barista training support.
- **Restaurants / Casual dining:** Often via foodservice distributors (Bidfood, Foodsite, Caterhub). Price-sensitive, monthly accounts. Switching costs low — if quality slips, they move.
- **Offices (corporate coffee):** Dominated by service providers who own the equipment (Nespresso Business, Delonghi, local service companies). They buy the service, not the bean. *White space: offices that want to source their own premium bean and own the equipment — smaller segment but higher margin.*
- **Hospitals, universities, mining camps:** Tend toward large tenders, commoditized product. Not Studex's target.

**Pain Points (from buyer surveys + LinkedIn Hostex data)**
1. **Reliability of supply** — load shedding disrupts SA roasting capacity. Buyers want a supplier who won't miss a Monday morning delivery.
2. **Traceability expectations rising** — hotel groups increasingly asked by guests "where is this coffee from?" Farm-level provenance is a sales tool now.
3. **Equipment + training捆绑** — buyers don't want to buy beans, they want a partner who manages the whole coffee experience.
4. **Price volatility** — arabica futures remain elevated ($2.50-3.00/lb range through 2026 per Rabobank). Buyers want fixed-price contracts or at least quarterly pricing, not monthly spot.
5. **Load shedding risk** — SA roasters running on generators pass costs to buyers. Rwandan green bean imported and roasted locally in SA avoids this = cost advantage.
